Our story
Questions, answered

The FAQ.

The things every PI asks on the first call, answered up front — because your time is better spent on the science.

How much does a lab website cost?

$1,499, flat. One-time fee for design and build — publications, people, and news pages, SEO, analytics, and your first month of care included. No surprise line items; additional work beyond scope is $99/hr and only ever with your approval.

How long does it take?

4–6 weeks from kickoff to launch. Most of your involvement is a kickoff call, one round of review, and sending us your papers and headshots. We know your calendar is the scarcest resource in the lab.

Can you rebuild our existing site instead of starting fresh?

Yes — most of our projects are rebuilds. We migrate everything worth keeping (publications, people, project pages), fix what's broken, and redesign around it. Nothing gets lost, including your search rankings.

Do we own the website?

Completely. Your domain, your code, your content, your analytics. If you leave, everything goes with you and keeps working. No proprietary platform, no hostage situation.

Who writes the content? We're scientists, not copywriters.

We do the heavy lifting. We draft your pages from your papers, CV, and a conversation — in plain language that's accurate enough for you and clear enough for a program officer. You review; we never publish anything you haven't approved. The science is never dumbed down, only opened up.

How do payments work with grants and university procurement?

However your institution needs: invoices, purchase orders, W-9 on request. Many labs pay from startup funds, discretionary accounts, or outreach budgets. The annual prepay option (10% off) fits neatly into a single budget line for grant reporting.

What happens if we cancel the care plan?

Your site keeps running — you own it. The care plan is month to month; pause it during a field season, restart when the papers come out. We'll be here.

Do you actually understand academia?

Our founder grew up in a working research lab — the child of a university scientist. We know what a preprint is, why the publications page must be perfect, what Reviewer 2 does to a person, and why your last site hasn't been updated since the postdoc who built it graduated.
